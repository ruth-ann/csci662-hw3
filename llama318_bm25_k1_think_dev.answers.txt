A classic economic concept!  After some thought, I believe the correct answer is:  Gresham's Law  Named after Sir Thomas Gresham, an English merchant and economist who lived in the 16th century. Gresham's Law states that "bad money drives out good", meaning that when a mixture of undervalued (or devalued) currency and overvalued (or valued) currency circulates together, the undervalued currency will drive out the overvalued one from circulation.  In simpler terms, if you have both old and new coins with different values circulating in the same economy, people tend to hoard the more valuable coins (the "good" money) and use the less valuable ones (the "bad" money), which reduces the amount of good money in circulation. Over time, this can lead to a devaluation of the entire currency system.  So, Gresham's Law is often cited as an explanation for why counterfeit or undervalued currency tends to drive out the more valued and authentic one.
